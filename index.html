Latin Pronunciation Guide for English Speakers

Spanish has five steady vowel phonemes and a small set of mostly consistent consonant patterns. This guide uses IPA and notes major variety differences: Peninsular/Castilian (northern–central Spain), Andalusian, Canarian, Mexican, Caribbean (Cuba, Puerto Rico, Dominican Republic), Colombian, Andean (Ecuador, Peru, Bolivia, Paraguay highlands), Rioplatense (Argentina, Uruguay), Chilean, and Central American.

Stress and Accent Marks

Spanish has no lexical tones. One syllable in each word carries stress. If a word ends in a vowel, n, or s, stress normally falls on the penultimate syllable; otherwise it falls on the final syllable. Written accents show exceptions or mark hiatus. Examples: cá-ma-ra [ˈka.ma.ɾa], cami-nó [ka.miˈno], pa-ís [paˈis].

Regional Notes at a Glance

Castilian (northern–central Spain): distinction /θ/ vs /s/, stronger /x/.
Andalusian: frequent /s/ aspiration or loss syllable-final, /x/ ≈ [h], occasional ch→[ʃ], d-dropping in participles.
Canarian: seseo /s/, /s/ aspiration, /x/ ≈ [h], Caribbean-like rhythm.
Mexican: clear /s/, /x/ often velar [x], conservative ch [t͡ʃ], stable vowels.
Caribbean (Cuba, PR, DR): /s/ aspiration or loss, word-final n→[ŋ], /x/ ≈ [h], syllable-final r may sound like [ɾ] or [h].
Colombian (highland): conservative consonants, clear /s/, careful tap vs trill.
Andean (Ecuador, Peru, Bolivia, Paraguay highlands): pockets of lleísmo /ʎ/, clear /s/, steady vowels.
Rioplatense (Argentina, Uruguay): y/ll as [ʒ] or [ʃ], intonation with Italian influence.
Chilean: tendency to weaken ch to [ʃ] in fast speech, some vowel reduction in casual speech.
Central American: mostly seseo, some /s/ aspiration on coasts, clear highland speech inland.

Helpful Symbols and Conventions

IPA brackets [ ] indicate detailed sounds. Slashes / / indicate phonemes. Doubled letters can indicate length in careful speech, but Spanish does not contrast vowel length. Intervocalic b, d, g are softened to [β, ð, ɣ]. The dieresis in vergüenza forces pronouncing u: [beɾˈɣwenθa] Castilian, [beɾˈɣwensa] elsewhere.
